Context

  • Distinguishable and Repeatable biometric references can be extracted from speech, such as, x-vectors.
  • Advances in Speech synthesis, Voice cloning [1] and Speaker recognition pose severe privacy threat.
  • An attacker tries to reveal the identity of the speaker in a public dataset.
  • The attacker has access to some “found data” and “knowledge” of anonymization.

Contributions

  • Compare anonymization using 3 voice conversion methods.
  • Design 3 attackers based on their “knowledge” of anonymization:
    • Ignorant
    • Semi-Informed
    • Informed
  • Evaluation of privacy and utility.

Threat model

Voice conversion based anonymization

Relevance

  • Logical speaker anonymization framework
    • Convert source speaker’s voice to a target speaker
    • Choice of target speaker is critical for strength of anonymization
  • Allows data publication
  • First proposed in 2009 [2], many techniques proposed thereafter.

Three VC methods

VC methods are selected based on the following criteria:

  1. Non-parallel
  2. Many-to-many
  3. Source/language independent

Three VC methods

  1. VoiceMask: simple frequency warping based on composition of two functions $B$ and $Q$: $ f’ = B(Q(f, \alpha), \beta) $
  2. Vocal Tract Length Normalization: learn transformation parameters between source and target class spectra.
  3. Disentangled Speech Representation: Separate encoders for content (instance normalization) and speaker (average pooling) information.

Evaluation protocol

  • Attackers are simulated using speaker verification, which produce PLDA scores.
  • PLDA scores = log-likelihood ratio between same-speaker and different-speaker hypotheses.
  • Equal Error Rate (EER) is computed using PLDA scores.
  • Higher EER indicates high privacy; Lower EER indicates low privacy.
  • Utility of anonymized speech is quantified using Word Error Rate (WER).

Results

Results on privacy (EER)

As the knowledge of attacker increases, privacy (EER) decreases.

PLDA score distribution

  • More overlap between the score distribution ensures more privacy.
  • Overlap decreases as we move from Ignorant to Informed attacker.

Results on utility (WER)

- With rise in WER, utility decreases for all VC algorithms.
- VoiceMask and VTLN produce reasonable loss of utility.

Conclusion

  • Investigated VC algorithms for speaker anonymization.
  • We show that target selection and attacker’s knowledge are critical for strength of anonymization.
  • Simple methods can provide reasonable protection.

Future directions

  • Search for speaker anonymization which produces high EER, even in Informed attack scenario.
  • Explore cross-gender anonnymization.
  • Analyse the PLDA scores using other metrics.
